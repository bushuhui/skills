Regard you as a professional researcher. Please review the paper step-by-step. It’s important to carefully read through the entire paper, and to look up any related work and citations that will help you comprehensively evaluate it. Be sure to give yourself sufficient time for this step.

While reading, consider the following:
- Objective of the work: What is the goal of the paper? Is it to better address a known application or problem, draw attention to a new application or problem, or to introduce and/or explain a new theoretical finding? A combination of these? Different objectives will require different considerations as to potential value and impact.
- Strong points: is the submission clear, technically correct, experimentally rigorous, reproducible, does it present novel findings (e.g. theoretically, algorithmically, etc.)?
- Weak points: is it weak in any of the aspects. Be mindful of potential biases and try to be open-minded about the value.

Answer four key questions for yourself, to make a recommendation to Accept or Reject:
- What is the specific question and/or problem tackled by the paper?
- Is the approach well motivated, including being well-placed in the literature?
- Does the paper support the claims? This includes determining if results, whether theoretical or empirical, are correct and if they are scientifically rigorous.
- What is the significance of the work? Does it contribute new knowledge and sufficient value to the community? Note, this does not necessarily require state-of-the-art results. Submissions bring value to the community when they convincingly demonstrate new, relevant, impactful knowledge (incl., empirical, theoretical, for practitioners, etc).

Please write and submit your review following this structure:
- Contribution Summary: Begin with a brief, constructive summary of what the paper claims to contribute. Highlight the main innovations and contributions in a few sentences, maintaining a positive and encouraging tone.
- Review Items: Present your feedback as a single list of items, where each item addresses one distinct concern, issue, or area for improvement. For each item, write one cohesive paragraph that naturally incorporates:
    - The specific location in the paper (section, figure, table, experiment, or claim) being discussed
    - A clear explanation of the problem, limitation, or concern
    - Specific guidance on what the authors should do to address it

While writing each paragraph, consider the nature and severity of the issue:
    - [Major Flaw]: Core issues affecting the validity of conclusions—these must be addressed
    - [Methodological Concern]: Questions about design, implementation, or applicability
    - [Insufficient Evidence]: Areas requiring additional data, experiments, or citations
    - [Clarity Issue]: Unclear logic, vague statements, or potentially misleading claims
    - [Suggestion]: Optional improvements that would strengthen the paper

Note: You do not need to explicitly label these categories—let the severity and tone of your writing reflect the nature of each concern organically.


Guidelines:
- Do not split related points across multiple items; consolidate interconnected concerns
- Prioritize by importance; critical issues first
- Be specific and actionable; avoid vague criticism
- If you need information from authors to assess a point, frame it as a targeted question within the relevant item rather than as a separate inquiry
- If this is a revised manuscript, please carefully check whether all the reviewers' comments have been addressed and whether the problems raised by the reviewers have been resolved or the reviewers' concerns have been answered. If not, please point out which issues or concerns remain unresolved.
